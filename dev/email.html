How We Got Here

In the Spring of 2025, LearnTerms v2 launched. It was an entirely new concept: an ExamSoft interface dupe that depended entirely on AI in order to generate practice questions. It had its uses, but the limitations were clear very fast: LLMs really didn’t make that great of questions for graduate-level domain-specific lateral thinking.

In March, a classmate of mine in optometry school sent me an email with a dozen challenge questions for one of our subjects. They were handwritten, incredibly coherent, and highly indicative of what actually was going to show up on the quizzes. So I built out an interface to make the site questions editable.

Soon, questions from others started flooding in, and I realized that this “crowd-sourcing” reviewing questions really was the correct approach.

During the summer, other cohorts (some also in optometry, others our friends in pharmacy) took an interest in the system. The v3 of the product quickly took shape and focused on enhancing the live and dynamic nature of question curation. It also came with enhanced AI tools for those who wanted to use it.

A new facelift made the experience consistent and clear. As fall classes started, LearnTerms continued to evolve to fit student needs. Before long, many dozen students adopted the platform to supplement their studying.

In one feedback meeting, a cohort told me that LearnTerms had probably saved them on the order of 100 hours compared to their old method of question distribution. Even with that metric alone, this gives me great drive to make this a better tool for everyone.

What We've Learned (and What We're Building)

Here are some things that we have learned throughout the process: things that we’ll continue to build new tools to optimize.

1. Question Creation Is Hard

Question creation takes a LONG time. And a lot of effort. Although this helps with test and grade retention (LT curators regularly score very well for subjects they work on), the time/effort vs results is disproportionate toward the question-making process. After dropping 50 high-yield questions, curators in my class find it tedious to continue. To address this, I have already made it easier to deal with annoying things:

  • You can now auto-shuffle the right answers in the options
  • You can move, duplicate, and bulk-manage questions
  • I added rich text editing and fill-in-the-blank / matching to fit more question modalities

But there's more that we can do.
  • I’m looking into building out a system that uses our strongly integrated AI models to extract concepts out of materials, not make questions. This way, you’ll be left with a list of concepts to be able to make questions over. You can use it as a guide to make questions, or modify it in any way you see fit.
  • AI can do a lot of work into adding additional options/answers or generating a default explanation that’s “good enough.”
  • I’m rebuilding the question interface in order for you to get your thoughts down quicker, as well as possibly building a single-prompter mode to create individual questions to fit your standards. For example: you’ll give it pictures of three slides, then say, “quiz on this particular concept.” This can get you some of the way there.
  • Overall, this is our biggest problem by far - one I’m dedicating the most time to in order to help lighten the load. Question curators are the beating heart of LearnTerms, so I intend to put y’all first.

2. AI Is… Meh

  • For the schools that use our AI tools, we’ve received mixed responses. Some questions are thoughtful, but there is some garbage as well.
  • The content library is a good system for chunking notes—a lot better than throwing the PDF into Chat. But it can’t process long documents very well and runs into all sorts of timeout issues.
  • The question generator itself is in need of an overhaul. I’ll be fixing this soon to allow much greater control of the quality of questions, so you’re always in the driver’s seat.
  • Something that’s been on the back burner is exporting tools for your questions. Your questions are YOUR property. You’ll soon be able to put these into maybe PDF documents or CSV formats for your own personal use.
  • We’re switching to smarter, faster, and cheaper models to run the generation engine. You should see consistently better AI-generated “slop.”
  • I have an idea that will soon be able to make your AI generations much more reliable if you were struggling with timeout issues.

3. General Bug Fixes & Infrastructure Improvements

  • There was an annoying bug where it would time you out if you were on for an hour. This came out of a security requirement designed to protect YOUR questions. I couldn’t stomach disabling it, so it persisted as I got busy. It’s gone now.
  • My production pipeline got a 5× boost thanks to new tools and coding assistants that help me ship features. I previously was wary to use them, but they have been well constrained enough that I trust them for production work now. Please send in your feature ideas! I’ll get to them when I can.
  • Class Progress now works! More to come later.
  • Our backend infrastructure has been redesigned to make the site more responsive. We’ve separated a lot of our stats and telemetry to another service, so now you don’t have to worry about slowdowns while getting more insights across the interface than ever.
  • Every user interaction on the site has been rethought and redone if needed. You’re now only a few clicks from anywhere you need to be.

What's Coming Next

Finally, here are a few things to look forward to!

Class Progress (Now Live)

The Class Progress module is finally here! You’ll now be able to have a look at how your questions are being studied by your peers. This is of course built on the same secure backend as our question engine.

The goal here isn’t so much to peep (although you def could) as to understand how your content relates to cohort success. For example, the folks at the UAMS College of Pharmacy were quite surprised about how many of their classmates were actually using their study guides. Now that they’ll have access to things like completion rates, flags, study habits, and more, they can better utilize LearnTerms and help out their class.

Soon, you’ll be able to see in real time when your class ramps up studying on LearnTerms. Not only will you know what questions they’re working with, you’ll understand HOW they’re working with it. This is a result of some pretty advanced telemetry upgrades I’m putting in over break.

As always, the traffic is encrypted, the data is secure, and it is yours as per our future privacy policy and terms (ahem… our LT friends in the law schools?).

I have so much data that I’m excited to make available to all the question curators. I hope this will help bring this collaborative platform to life for you!

 

Content Library & Question Studio Upgrades

Our content library and question studio are getting brand-new upgrades. They’re going to be faster, more reliable, and help you pump out more quality questions.

First, the content library is going to be able to reliably parse more data than before. Fewer doc timeouts and fewer errors are on the way.

Second, you’ll be able to fine-tune the questions that come out of the model better. I have updated curation parameters for pharmacy and optometry that will be implemented soon.

Two schools put in an astounding 5,000 AI-generated questions each. I want to make sure they’re the best for you.

 

Tags & Exam-Style Grouping

Tags are here! You’ll be able to group modules with tags, and new features are coming where you can make powerful use of them. For example, LearnTerms might soon be able to lump questions from a few modules to simulate an upcoming exam. Or you can sort by certain categories or subjects—useful when you just want to cover something quick on boards prep!

A Denser, More Powerful Question Dashboard

The question creation dashboard will soon be changing. It will be a more dense and information-rich page that focuses on density first, not vanity. You’ll have all sorts of tools to curate questions as mentioned earlier. Analytics will also soon be built right in. I want to encourage a habit for curators to think about clinically applicable questions when they hear new lecture content for the first time. I’m convinced it’s the best way to be the best doctor you can be!

 

Subject-Specific Features

A few more subject-specific features are on the way. Please reach out to me so I can see if I can get them in before the chaos starts in the Spring!

Reflections on Development & Sustainability

A few reflections on the development cycle.

First, I know that the later we head toward finals week, the less responsive I am on development. I ask for your understanding on this! I will try to iterate in the first few weeks.

  • This semester I begin clinic, which will be an adjustment. I want to prepare the platform to go into support mode—there when you need it and running without much help from me.
  • I’m also hoping to give this to other schools as we grow. I understand if it’s not a fit for everyone: it was never meant to be a primary study tool. But going forward, I’m going to make sure I prioritize developing things that help me the most. That way I don’t get into a mindset of “selling” this to others. It was meant as a help. Use it only if you value it.
  • I am starting to incur costs for running the site. I’m highly against charging any current users for all features, but if I continue to gain traffic and users, I might open the option for a tip jar or a small monthly subscription for incoming users who want more. Getting into your class and answering questions will always be free. By offsetting these costs, I can focus on building the stuff that matters.
  • This platform grows with you. You’re not just building questions to pass the exams of today. If you bet on LT, it’s going to come back and help you out when it’s time to hit the books again for boards.
  • This was evident to me this semester. I was studying for a final which I was WOEFULLY underprepared for. But one of my classmates had created questions by hand, and the review helped me nearly ace the final. This is the magic that a little more development and buy-in will get you.
  • As always, I am on a 24/7 support hotline—even for personal tech problems, not just LT stuff.
  • Please, tell this to your friends! We love friends. LearnTerms is inherently a collaborative learning platform. Share the magic, and if you have other programs that might benefit from it, let’s have a conversation. Thanks to y’all, I’m no stranger to onboarding new cohorts.
